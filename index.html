Baby Squirrel Care Guide

Complete feeding instructions and care tips for orphaned baby squirrels in India

1. Important Notes Before Starting

Warmth First

Baby squirrels are fragile. Keep them warm (around 99°F) before feeding. Use a heating pad set on low or a warm water bottle wrapped in cloth.

Feeding Tools

Use a small dropper, syringe, or soft nipple for feeding. Sterilize all equipment before each use to prevent bacterial infections.

Formula Temperature

Never feed cold formula or milk — it must be slightly warm (around 98°F). Test on your wrist like for a human baby.

Buffalo Milk

Buffalo milk should always be diluted (more on that below). Undiluted milk can cause digestive issues and diarrhea.

Weaning

At 6+ weeks, slowly start introducing solid foods. Monitor their reaction to new foods and introduce one at a time.

2. Age-wise Roadmap & Feeding Timetable

Age (Weeks) Milk Feeding Other Foods Frequency
4–5 Weeks Buffalo milk diluted with warm water (3 parts milk + 1 part water). Add a pinch of honey if needed for taste. None yet. Only milk. Every 3–4 hours (5–6 times/day). Approximately 2-4ml per feeding.
5–6 Weeks Same diluted milk, slightly thicker (4:1 milk-water ratio). Can add a drop of vitamin supplement if available. Tiny pieces of banana, apple, or soft soaked bread. Introduce slowly in pea-sized amounts. Milk: 4–5 times/day.
Fruits: 1–2 times/day (tiny amounts).
6–7 Weeks Thicker milk (almost undiluted but still slightly warm). Can begin introducing puppy milk replacer as alternative. Add peanuts (without skin), soaked almonds, and tiny bits of roti/chapati. Always provide fresh water. Milk: 3–4 times/day.
Solids: 2–3 times/day.
7–8 Weeks Start reducing milk, increase solids. Transition to water in shallow dish. More fruits (papaya, guava, apple), dry fruits (almonds, cashews), cooked rice, and small grains. Milk: 2 times/day.
Solids: 3–4 times/day.

3. Daily Feeding Timetable Example

For a 4–5 week-old (mostly milk)

  • 6:30 AM – Warm diluted milk (2-3ml)
  • 10:00 AM – Warm diluted milk (2-3ml)
  • 1:00 PM – Warm diluted milk (2-3ml)
  • 4:00 PM – Warm diluted milk (2-3ml)
  • 7:00 PM – Warm diluted milk (2-3ml)
  • 10:30 PM – Warm diluted milk (last feed before sleep)

Never overfeed - stomach should feel rounded but not tight

For a 7–8 week-old (more solids)

  • 7:00 AM – Small piece of banana or apple
  • 9:00 AM – Milk feed (warm, reduced quantity)
  • 12:00 PM – Small roti pieces with tiny mashed papaya
  • 3:00 PM – Few soaked almonds or peanuts
  • 6:00 PM – Milk feed (less quantity)
  • 9:00 PM – A small piece of apple or chapati

Always remove uneaten fresh food after 2-3 hours

4. Foods Easily Available in Indian Homes

Fruits

Banana (mashed), apple (grated), guava (seeds removed), papaya (mashed), chikoo (mashed)

Nuts

Peanuts (unsalted, crushed), almonds (soaked 12hrs, peeled, crushed), cashews (unsalted, crushed)

Grains

Cooked rice (soft), roti (soft pieces, soaked in milk if needed), soaked bread (in diluted milk)

Vegetables

Carrot (finely shredded), boiled corn (kernels only, after 7 weeks), cucumber (small pieces)

Others

Diluted buffalo milk (3:1 ratio), honey (small pinch for taste if needed), electrolyte water (for hydration)

5. Weaning & Care Tips

  • By 8–9 weeks, the squirrel will start eating more solids and less milk. Offer variety to ensure balanced nutrition.
  • Always provide fresh drinking water in a small, shallow dish that they can't tip over.
  • Keep them clean and warm in a soft cloth nest. Change bedding daily to maintain hygiene.
  • Avoid chocolate, spicy foods, or salted snacks — toxic for squirrels. Also avoid citrus fruits and onions.

Additional Care Tip

As the squirrel grows, provide safe branches for chewing and climbing to help with natural behavior development. Always supervise interactions with other pets. Squirrels need mental stimulation, so provide toys and opportunities to forage for food.

Health Monitoring

Regularly check for signs of illness: lethargy, loss of appetite, diarrhea, or difficulty breathing. Baby squirrels can dehydrate quickly, so monitor their hydration by gently pinching the skin - if it doesn't snap back quickly, they may need fluids.
